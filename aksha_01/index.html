Opinion

India’s health, economic prosperity and social development are closely interconnected. Over the years, the country has made significant gains on this front. We are witnessing declining trends in Infant Mortality Rates (IMR) and Under-Five Mortality Rate (U5MR) – key proxies for a nation’s health status. Polio-free in 2014, India has now rolled out an intensified mission to improve vaccine coverage. Technology-based interventions, in areas like TB detection, that enable ‘here-and-now’ review of a health program’s impact, are in place. Frontline health workers are being equipped with digital tools to monitor nutrition levels and provide a real-time report on the health of their communities. Building an improved sanitation eco-system – a necessity for sustained improvement India’s health - has also been prioritized in recent years. These new initiatives and technology-based approaches to healthcare are expected to be game-changing and give us many reasons to be optimistic.

But much more needs to be done if ambitious national health outcomes are to be fully realized. These include, among others, enabling good health and nutrition for children to ensure they reach their full intellectual capability; focusing on eliminating diseases such as tuberculosis, that disproportionately affect the poor; and a continuous effort to close the access, affordability and quality gap in health services for the poor and marginalized communities across India.

What will it take to step up the gains made so far? Where will the resources come from? Which proven strategies can be scaled so that they reach more people and save more lives? Here is a thought-provoking article by Bill Gates (first published in the Wall Street Journal) , that provides evidence from around the world suggesting that investing in health provides the surest route out of poverty. The write up also highlights that governments must continue to replenish investments in successful interventions so that the progress made on national and global health agendas remain on course.
